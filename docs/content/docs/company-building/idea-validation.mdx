> While some founders can afford to invest heavily in building first, most should validate their ideas before significant development. Find the right balance for your situation, but lean towards validation if resources are limited.

## Pre-Build Checklist

Before you even start building your MVP, ask yourself these **mandatory** questions:

- **Is it a painkiller or a vitamin?** - Painkillers solve urgent problems people will pay for. Vitamins are nice-to-haves that are hard to monetize.
- **Do you have an unfair advantage?** - What makes you uniquely qualified to solve this problem better than anyone else? Do you know someone in this space or you have special knowledge that others don't?
- **Can you test it &lt;1 month?** - If validation takes longer than a month, you're overthinking it. Start simple.
- **Do you know how to get the first 10 users?** - Have a concrete plan for reaching your initial customers.
- **Which metric defines your success?** - Pick ONE key metric that proves your idea works.

<Note>
**Critical**: If you can't answer these questions confidently, **don't start building yet**. Spend more time validating first.
</Note>

## Start With Your Network First

**Before using idea finder tools, tap into your existing network:**

- **Friends and family** - Ask about their daily frustrations and business challenges
- **Industry connections** - Colleagues, former coworkers, professional contacts
- **Local businesses** - Coffee shops, restaurants, service providers in your area
- **Community groups** - Meetups, clubs, religious organizations, hobby groups

**Why this works better:**
- ✅ **Real people** with **real problems** you can solve immediately
- ✅ **Built-in trust** and willingness to give honest feedback
- ✅ **Direct access** to validate and iterate quickly
- ✅ **Potential first customers** who already know and trust you

<Note>
**Start here first** - you're more likely to find viable ideas from people you already know than from generic idea databases. Only use idea finder tools if your network doesn't yield promising opportunities.
</Note>

Many of your first users will simply be word of mouth, especially if you don't have a large online presence. That's okay. Don't be afraid to speak to potential customers in person, cold-dm, cold-email, or even cold-call. If your product solves a genuine pain point, people will be happy to help you.

## Idea Finder Tools

<Callout type="warn" title="Important Advice for Solo Founders">

If you're building your first couple of products:

**Don't try to come up with a new idea, just build a different version of an already successful idea**

You can just:
- Make it cheaper
- Make it faster
- Make it better
- Make it more convenient
- Make it more accessible
- Make it more user-friendly
- Make it more efficient

Your chances of success are much higher if you do this.
</Callout>

## Rapid Iteration Strategy

**The Rapid Iteration Approach:**

- **Week 1-2**: Test your idea with a simple landing page
- **No traction?** Kill it and start another idea
- **Multiple bets**: You don't have to stick with a single idea. Do this for multiple ideas.
- **Double down**: Only when an idea shows traction (waitlist signups, genuine interest)

<Note>
**Remember**: You just need to ge **one idea** off the ground. Then you can start proper development.
</Note>
